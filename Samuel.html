Arthur Samuel

Pioneer of Machine Learning & AI Gaming

An interactive presentation on the man who taught computers how to learn.

Early Life & Engineering Career

  • Born in Emporia, Kansas, in 1901.
  • He studied Electrical Engineering at MIT and later taught there.
  • He had a long, distinguished career at Bell Labs and then IBM, where he worked on early computer hardware.
Watch: The Dawn of Computing at IBM

Major Contribution: The Checkers Program

  • In the 1950s, he created one of the world's first self-learning programs.
  • The program learned to play checkers by playing against itself and human opponents thousands of times.
  • It continuously improved its strategy, eventually becoming a better player than Samuel himself.
  • This was a groundbreaking, public demonstration of a machine "learning."
Samuel's early checkers program

Coining "Machine Learning"

  • In 1959, Arthur Samuel popularized the term **"Machine Learning."**
  • He defined it as the field that gives computers the ability to learn without being explicitly programmed.
  • His program used pioneering techniques like **alpha-beta pruning**, a search algorithm still used in AI today.
  • His work proved that a machine could develop skills and strategies on its own.

Influence on Modern-Day AI

The Blueprint for Learning

  • His checkers program is the direct ancestor of modern game-playing AIs like Deep Blue and AlphaGo.
  • The concept of a machine improving through self-play is now a core part of **Reinforcement Learning**.
  • His definition of machine learning is still the standard used today.
  • He showed that complex "intelligent" behavior could come from simple learning rules.
