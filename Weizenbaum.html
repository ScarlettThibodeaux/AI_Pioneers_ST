Joseph Weizenbaum

Creator of ELIZA & AI's First Critic

An interactive presentation on the man who created the first chatbot and warned of AI's dangers.

Early Life & Escape to a New World

  • Born in Berlin, Germany, in 1923 to a Jewish family.
  • He and his family fled Nazi Germany in 1936, settling in the United States.
  • He studied mathematics and worked on some of the earliest digital computers in the 1950s before becoming a professor at MIT.
MIT's Role in Early AI

Major Contribution: ELIZA

  • In 1966, he created **ELIZA**, one of the world's first chatbots.
  • ELIZA simulated a Rogerian psychotherapist by recognizing keywords in user input and turning the phrases back as questions.
  • He intended it to be a parody, to show how superficial human-to-computer communication was.
  • To his surprise, people confided in ELIZA, forming emotional attachments to the simple program.

AI's Conscience: The "ELIZA Effect"

  • He was troubled by how easily people confused simulation with real understanding. He called this the **"ELIZA effect."**
  • This experience turned him into one of AI's most important critics.
  • In his 1976 book, *Computer Power and Human Reason*, he warned against giving computers tasks that require wisdom, empathy, and judgment.
  • He argued there are some roles machines should never play, regardless of their capability.

Influence on Modern-Day AI

A Lasting Warning

  • ELIZA is the direct ancestor of every modern chatbot, from virtual assistants to large language models.
  • The "ELIZA effect" is a central problem in AI ethics today, as we interact with increasingly convincing AI.
  • His questions about the difference between calculation and judgment are more relevant than ever.
  • He reminds us to think critically about what roles we want AI to have in our lives.
